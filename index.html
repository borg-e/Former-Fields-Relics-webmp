Former Fields Relicts: European synthesis Map

Reconstructing the history of land use, and thereby assessing the ecological legacies associated with past practices, is a key prerequisite for effective environmental governance (Ellis, 2021). [cite: 55, 56, 57] Former agricultural landforms —often organized within former field systems (FFS)— are widely preserved across Europe. [cite: 57, 58] Among the most common micro-topographies described in the literature are lynchets, terraces, ridge-and-furrow systems, stone walls and headland ridges. [cite: 58, 59] LiDAR has enabled numerous regional studies on landscape history and heritage, including vast areas now covered by forest. [cite: 59, 60] On the other hand, geoarchaeological surveys have provided an initial, though still incomplete, chronological framework. [cite: 60, 61] This study encompasses the temperate transition zone where oceanic and continental influences met. [cite: 61, 62] The scope also aims to follow the historical diffusion of the plow, tracing its path from its Central European origins to Alsace and Lorraine (Haudricourt and Delamarre, 1988). [cite: 62, 63] Furthermore, it extends to the North-Western French openfields, spanning from the Paris Basin to the Beauce region, to ensure a comprehensive analysis of these traditional agricultural landscapes.
